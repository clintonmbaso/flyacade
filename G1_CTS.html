Types of Lines
There are various types of lines used in art and design, each serving a different purpose and creating a unique visual effect. Here are details on some common types of lines:

1. Straight Line: A straight line is simple and unbroken, extending from one point to another without curves. It can convey stability, order, and precision.
Examples: Ruler-edge lines, the edge of a book.

2. Curved Line: A curved line is gentle and flows smoothly, often forming a continuous curve. It can suggest grace, movement, and fluidity.
Examples: Circles, arcs, and waves.

G1CTST2L01 – Exploring Paper Crafts
Sunday, April 02, 2023
Objective:
Students will explore the art of paper crafting by creating their own paper masks and greeting cards, fostering creativity and fine motor skills development.

Materials Needed:
- Colored construction paper
- Markers, crayons, or colored pencils
- Scissors
- Glue sticks
- Decorative items (e.g., stickers, sequins, googly eyes)
- Cardstock or thick paper for greeting cards
- Clay (optional, for advanced students)

Lesson Duration:
45-60 minutes

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by discussing what craft is and the different types of crafts.
2. Explain that today, we will be exploring paper crafts, where we'll create masks and greeting cards.

Activity 1: Paper Masks (20 minutes):
1. Show examples of paper masks to the class.
2. Distribute colored construction paper and scissors to each student.
3. Instruct students to fold their paper in half vertically (like a book).
4. Guide them in drawing the outline of a mask on one side of the paper, ensuring it touches the folded edge.
5. Assist students in cutting out their mask shape, ensuring they do not cut along the folded edge.
6. Once cut, help them carefully unfold their paper to reveal the mask shape.
7. Encourage students to decorate their masks using markers, crayons, and any other decorative items provided.
8. Allow time for students to share their creations with the class.

Activity 2: Greeting Cards (20 minutes):
1. Provide each student with a piece of cardstock or thick paper, folded in half to create a card.
2. Discuss the purpose of greeting cards and when people typically give them.
3. Guide students in decorating the front of their cards with various designs, using markers, crayons, or colored pencils.
4. Encourage them to write a thoughtful message inside their cards, such as "Happy Birthday" or "Thank You."
5. Assist students in adding any additional decorations, such as stickers or sequins.
6. Allow time for students to exchange their cards with classmates or take them home to share with family.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Gather the class together to reflect on the activities.
2. Ask students what they enjoyed most about creating their paper crafts.
3. Emphasize the importance of creativity and self-expression in art.
4. Encourage students to continue exploring different types of crafts at home.

Extension Activity (Optional):
For advanced students or those interested in sculpting, provide clay and sculpting tools for them to create small sculptures. Encourage them to use their imagination to shape the clay into animals, objects, or characters.

G1CTST2L03 – Exploring Rhythm and Beat
Sunday, April 02, 2023
Objective:
Students will explore the concepts of rhythm and beat through clapping, tapping, and playing simple percussion instruments like tambourines or rhythm sticks, fostering an understanding of musical patterns and sequences.

Materials Needed:
- Tambourines
- Rhythm sticks
- Optional: Other simple percussion instruments (e.g., hand drums, maracas)
- Music player or device with speakers
- Recorded music with clear rhythm and beat (e.g., nursery rhymes, children's songs)

Lesson Duration:
45-60 minutes

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by discussing what rhythm and beat are in music.
2. Explain that today, we will be exploring rhythm and beat through clapping, tapping, and playing percussion instruments.

Activity 1: Clapping and Tapping (20 minutes):
1. Play a piece of recorded music with a clear beat, such as a nursery rhyme or children's song.
2. Lead students in clapping along to the beat of the music, emphasizing the steady rhythm.
3. Encourage students to tap their feet or other body parts in time with the beat as well.
4. Pause the music periodically and ask students to describe what they notice about the rhythm and beat.
5. Demonstrate different rhythms by clapping patterns (e.g., long-short, short-short-long) and have students mimic them.

Activity 2: Playing Percussion Instruments (20 minutes):
1. Distribute tambourines, rhythm sticks, or other simple percussion instruments to students.
2. Play another piece of recorded music with a clear beat.
3. Instruct students to play along with the beat using their percussion instruments, following the rhythm of the music.
4. Encourage students to experiment with different ways of playing their instruments (e.g., shaking the tambourine, tapping the rhythm sticks).
5. Pause the music periodically and ask students to share their experiences playing along with the beat.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Gather the class together to reflect on the activities.
2. Ask students what they learned about rhythm and beat from the lesson.
3. Emphasize the importance of listening carefully and staying in time with the music when playing percussion instruments.
4. Encourage students to continue exploring rhythm and beat at home by clapping, tapping, or playing along with their favorite songs.

Extension Activity (Optional):
For advanced students or those interested in further exploring rhythm and beat, introduce more complex patterns and sequences of sounds using a variety of percussion instruments. Encourage students to create their own rhythms and beats using the instruments provided.

G1CTST2L04 – Introduction to Basic Music Notation
Tuesday, December 19, 2023
Objective:
Students will begin to familiarize themselves with basic music symbols, such as notes and rests, and understand the concept of musical notation, including how notes represent duration.

Materials Needed:
- Large whiteboard or poster paper
- Markers
- Visual aids of music notes and rests
- Optional: Musical instruments (e.g., keyboard, xylophone) for demonstration purposes

Lesson Duration:
45-60 minutes

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Start by discussing the importance of music notation in communicating musical ideas.
2. Explain that today, we will begin to learn about basic music symbols and how they represent sounds and silence in music.

Activity 1: Introduction to Music Symbols (20 minutes):
1. Display visual aids of basic music symbols, including notes and rests, on the whiteboard or poster paper.
2. Explain each symbol to the students, emphasizing their names and how they represent different durations of sound or silence.
3. Demonstrate the duration of each symbol by tapping or clapping rhythms, using the visual aids as a guide.
4. Have students practice identifying and naming each music symbol, both individually and as a class.
5. Encourage students to ask questions and clarify any confusion about the symbols.

Activity 2: Creating Rhythms (20 minutes):
1. Divide the class into small groups or pairs.
2. Provide each group with a set of rhythm cards or flashcards depicting various combinations of music notes and rests.
3. Instruct students to work together to arrange the cards to create their own rhythms.
4. Encourage students to experiment with different combinations of notes and rests to create interesting rhythms.
5. Allow each group to share their rhythm creations with the class, either by performing them orally or using simple percussion instruments to play them.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Gather the class together to reflect on the activities.
2. Ask students what they learned about basic music notation and symbols.
3. Emphasize the importance of understanding music notation in playing and reading music.
4. Encourage students to continue practicing and exploring music notation at home.

Extension Activity (Optional):
For advanced students or those interested in further exploring music notation, introduce more complex rhythms and symbols, such as dotted notes and time signatures. Provide opportunities for students to practice reading and writing music notation using worksheets or interactive online tools.

G1CTST2L09 – Understanding Levers
Sunday, April 02, 2023
Objective:
Students will learn about levers as a type of simple machine, understanding their components and how they are used to lift, push, or balance loads.

Materials Needed:
- Visual aids (pictures or diagrams) representing levers
- Optional: Simple lever models for demonstration purposes (e.g., seesaw, crowbar)

Lesson Duration:
30-45 minutes

Introduction (5 minutes):
1. Begin by discussing what simple machines are and why they are important in everyday life.
2. Introduce the concept of levers as one of the primary types of simple machines.

Activity 1: Explaining the Components of a Lever (15 minutes):
1. Show visual aids (pictures or diagrams) representing levers, highlighting their components: the rigid bar (beam), pivot point (fulcrum), and force arms (input and output).
2. Explain to students that levers work by using a pivot point (fulcrum) to amplify or redirect a force, making it easier to perform work.
3. Demonstrate how levers are used to lift, push, or balance loads in various real-life examples, such as seesaws, crowbars, and the human forearm.
4. Discuss the different types of levers (first-class, second-class, and third-class) and their characteristics.

Activity 2: Identifying Levers in Everyday Objects (15 minutes):
1. Provide students with pictures or examples of everyday objects that use levers, such as scissors, pliers, or a hammer.
2. Encourage students to identify the components of each lever (beam, fulcrum, input and output force arms) and discuss how they function.
3. Ask students to brainstorm other examples of levers they may encounter in their daily lives, such as doors, wheelbarrows, or playground equipment.

Conclusion (5 minutes):
1. Gather the class together to review what they learned about levers.
2. Ask students to share examples of levers they identified and how they work.
3. Emphasize the importance of levers in making work easier and more efficient.
4. Encourage students to be observant and look for levers in the world around them.

Extension Activity (Optional):
For an extended lesson or as a follow-up activity, invite students to create their own simple lever models using everyday materials such as rulers, pencils, and small objects for weights. Encourage them to experiment with different fulcrum positions and observe how it affects the operation of the lever.
