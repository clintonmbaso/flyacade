B1EngT1L01 – Understanding Syllogisms
Sunday, April 02, 2023

Objective: Introduce students to the concept of syllogisms and help them understand the basic structure.

Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed examples of syllogisms

Lesson Steps:

1. Introduction (10 minutes):
   - Begin with a simple question: "Have you ever wondered how we make conclusions or predictions about things?" Discuss student responses.

2. Definition of Syllogism (15 minutes):
   - Present the formal definition of syllogism: "Syllogism is a way of reasoning where conclusions are drawn from two given or assumed propositions, known as premises."
   - Write this definition on the whiteboard and break it down for easier understanding.

3. Interactive Example (20 minutes):
   - Use a familiar scenario like "All dogs bark, Max is a dog, therefore Max barks" to illustrate the three parts of a syllogism: major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.
   - Engage students in constructing their own simple syllogisms.

4. Class Practice (15 minutes):
   - Provide printed examples of syllogisms involving everyday situations. Have students work in pairs to identify the premises and conclusions.

5. Discussion and Clarifications (10 minutes):
   - Discuss the examples as a class, addressing any questions or misconceptions.
   - Clarify the difference between major and minor premises.

6. Homework (5 minutes):
   - Assign simple syllogism exercises for homework to reinforce understanding.

B1EngT1L04 – Applying Syllogisms to Everyday Scenarios
Sunday, October 22, 2023

Objective: Enable students to apply syllogistic reasoning to real-life situations.

Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Chart paper
- Printed examples of real-life syllogisms

Lesson Steps:

1. Recap (10 minutes):
   - Begin by reviewing the definition of syllogism from the previous lesson.

2. Introduction to Real-life Scenarios (15 minutes):
   - Discuss how syllogisms can be used to make predictions or draw conclusions in everyday life.
   - Provide examples like "If it's sunny, we'll go to the park. It's sunny today, so we'll go to the park."

3. Class Brainstorming (20 minutes):
   - Engage students in brainstorming real-life scenarios where syllogistic reasoning could be applied.
   - List these scenarios on the whiteboard or chart paper.

4. Group Activity (15 minutes):
   - Divide the class into small groups and assign each group a real-life scenario.
   - Ask them to create a syllogism based on their scenario.

5. Presentations (10 minutes):
   - Have each group present their syllogism to the class, explaining the major and minor premises and the conclusion.

6. Discussion and Reflection (10 minutes):
   - Discuss the presented scenarios as a class. Encourage reflection on how syllogisms can be useful in various situations.

7. Homework Assignment (5 minutes):
   - Assign homework where students identify syllogisms in news articles, advertisements, or other real-world examples.

These lesson plans aim to progressively introduce and apply the concept of syllogisms, moving from basic definitions to practical applications in everyday scenarios.

Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Printed examples of syllogisms

Lesson Steps:

   - Start with a brief recap of the previous lesson on syllogism introduction. Ask students if they remember what a syllogism is.

   - Reinforce the definition of syllogism and emphasize its role in reasoning and drawing conclusions.
