Let Us Read
In the very beginning, there was nothing but darkness and quiet. But then, God decided to create something amazing.

On the first day, God said, "Let there be light!" And just like that, there was light. God separated the light from the darkness, calling the light "day" and the darkness "night." That was the first day.

On the second day, God said, "Let there be a beautiful sky above the Earth." And there it was, a clear blue sky.

On the third day, God said, "Let there be land and vegetation." So, there was land and water, and God saw that it was good. He made the land green with grass, colorful flowers, and tall trees.

On the fourth day, God said, "Let there be the sun to shine in the day, the moon to glow at night, and the stars to twinkle in the sky." And so, the sun, moon, and stars lit up the world.

On the fifth day, God said, "Let there be creatures in the water and birds in the sky." The oceans teemed with fish, and the sky filled with birds of all kinds.

On the sixth day, God created animals to roam the land. Lions, elephants, giraffes, and so many more. But God had one more special creation in mind. He said, "Let's make people in our own image." And with that, God created Adam, the first man, and Eve, the first woman. They were to take care of the Earth and all the creatures on it.

God looked at everything He had made and was very pleased. It was all so wonderful. And on the seventh day, God rested.

That's the beautiful story of how God created the world. Everything in it is a gift, and it's up to us to take care of this amazing creation.

And so, children, remember to enjoy the beauty of the world and be kind to all the creatures in it. That's the special job God gave us when He created everything.

The First People
Thursday, November 09, 2023

A long time ago, in a beautiful garden called Eden, God created the first man, Adam, and the first woman, Eve. They were the first people on Earth, and they had everything they needed in the Garden of Eden.

God gave Adam and Eve one special rule: They could eat from any tree in the garden, except for one, the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil. God said, "You must not eat from that tree, or you will surely die."

Adam and Eve were happy in the garden, taking care of the animals and enjoying all the fruits and plants. They had a perfect life, but one day, a crafty serpent approached Eve and said, "Did God really say you couldn't eat from any tree?"

Eve replied, "We can eat from any tree except the one in the middle of the garden, or we will die."

The serpent tempted Eve, saying, "You won't die. If you eat from that tree, you'll become wise, just like God." Eve looked at the tree, and she saw that its fruit looked good to eat and would make her wise. So, she took a bite and gave some to Adam.

As soon as they ate the fruit, they realized they had done something wrong. They felt ashamed and hid from God. But God knew what had happened, and He asked them about it. Adam and Eve admitted their mistake and were sorry for disobeying God's rule.

God explained that because of their choice, there would be consequences. Life would become more challenging, and they would no longer live in the perfect garden. However, God still loved them and made clothes for them to wear.

Although Adam and Eve had to leave the Garden of Eden, they started a new life outside with God's guidance. The story of the Fall of Adam and Eve teaches us about making choices, listening to God's guidance, and the importance of honesty and responsibility. It also reminds us that God forgives us when we make mistakes and helps us start anew.

The Fall of Man
Friday, November 24, 2023

In the heart of Eden, where joy and innocence danced in harmony, a whisper of temptation slithered into the ears of Eve. One day, as she wandered near the Tree of Knowledge, a crafty serpent coiled itself around a branch and spoke, "Did God really say you couldn't eat from any tree in the garden?"

Eve hesitated, her curiosity piqued. "We can eat from any tree except the one in the middle, or we will die," she replied, repeating God's warning.

The serpent, with a sly smile, twisted the truth like a mischievous riddle. "You won't surely die! Eat the fruit, and you'll be like God, knowing good and evil."

The forbidden fruit hung low on the branch, its allure too tempting for Eve to resist. She plucked it, took a bite, and shared it with Adam, who stood beside her.

In that moment, a profound change occurred. Their eyes were opened, and they saw the world with a new understanding. Yet, the knowledge came with a heavy price - a realization of their nakedness and a sense of guilt.

Fearfully, they confessed their disobedience. The consequences rippled through Eden like a somber melody. God, in His justice, spoke of the hardships that would now accompany life - pain in childbirth, toil in cultivating the land, and the awareness of both good and evil.

Yet, even in this moment of consequence, God's love shone through. He made garments for Adam and Eve, symbolizing His continued care and providing a glimmer of hope amidst the shadows of their disobedience.

The fall of man marked a turning point in the story of creation, introducing humanity to the complexities of choice, consequence, and the enduring grace of a loving Creator.

The Brother's Choices

A long time ago, in the early days of the world, there were two brothers named Cain and Abel. They were the sons of Adam and Eve, the first people created by God.

Cain was a farmer, and he worked hard to grow crops. Abel was a shepherd, and he looked after the sheep. Both brothers wanted to please God and show their love for Him.

One day, they both brought offerings to God. Abel brought the best of his lambs as an offering, while Cain offered some of his crops. God was pleased with Abel's gift, but He did not accept Cain's offering. This made Cain very angry and jealous of his brother.

God saw Cain's anger and said to him, "Why are you so upset, Cain? If you do what is right, you will be accepted. But if you don't, sin is crouching at your door, and you must master it."

However, Cain did not listen to God's advice. Instead, he invited Abel to go out into the field with him. There, in a fit of jealousy and anger, Cain attacked Abel and killed him.

When God asked Cain where Abel was, he lied and said he didn't know. But God knew the truth. He told Cain that Abel's blood cried out to Him from the ground.

As a consequence of his actions, Cain was punished by God. He became a wanderer on the Earth and was marked so that others would not harm him.

The story of Cain and Abel teaches us about the choices we make and the consequences of our actions. It shows the importance of doing what is right, controlling our anger, and not letting jealousy or hatred take over our hearts. It also reminds us that God sees everything, and it's always better to be truthful and make good choices.

The story of the sacrifice of Isaac is a powerful illustration of trust, obedience, and the profound tests that come with a life of faith. 12 Sons of Isreal
Friday, November 24, 2023

Jacob's twelve sons, born to him through his wives Leah and Rachel, as well as their maidservants Bilhah and Zilpah, played significant roles in shaping the destiny of the Israelite tribes. Here is a brief overview of Jacob's twelve sons:

1. Reuben: Jacob's firstborn, but he lost the birthright due to his indiscretion. Reuben slept with Bilhah, his father's concubine.

2. Simeon and Levi: These two brothers were known for their impulsive and vengeful nature. They avenged their sister Dinah's honor by tricking the men of Shechem into a covenant and then attacking them.

3. Judah: Judah became a prominent figure among the brothers. It was from the tribe of Judah that the kings of Israel descended, including King David and, ultimately, Jesus Christ.

4. Dan and Naphtali: Sons of Bilhah, these brothers contributed to the twelve tribes. Little is mentioned about them before Joseph's story.

5. Gad and Asher: Sons of Zilpah, they also played roles in the formation of the tribes.

6. Issachar and Zebulun: Sons of Leah, they are listed among the brothers.

7. Joseph: Jacob's favorite son, born to Rachel. His coat of many colors and dreams of prominence incited jealousy among his brothers. They sold him into slavery, which marked the beginning of Joseph's journey to Egypt.

8. Benjamin: The youngest son, born to Rachel. His birth was accompanied by Rachel's death. Benjamin became dearly important to Jacob.

These twelve sons went on to become the patriarchs of the twelve tribes of Israel. Their individual stories, along with the events leading to Joseph's sale into slavery, contributed to the intricate narrative of the Israelite people and their journey through history.

The Plagues of Egypt
Thursday, November 09, 2023

A long time ago, in a land called Egypt, the people of Israel were suffering. They were slaves and had to do hard work for the king of Egypt, who was called Pharaoh. But God had a plan to set them free, and He chose a man named Moses to help.

Moses went to Pharaoh and said, "Let my people go!" But Pharaoh refused to listen, so God sent ten plagues, one after another, to show His power and convince Pharaoh to let the Israelites go.

The plagues were:

1. Water Turned to Blood: The waters of the Nile River turned into blood, and all the fish died. But Pharaoh's heart was still hard.

2. Frogs: Frogs swarmed all over Egypt, making it very unpleasant. But Pharaoh would not change his mind.

3. Gnats or Lice: Tiny insects called gnats or lice filled the land. Pharaoh's magicians couldn't stop them.

4. Flies: Swarms of flies filled the air, and it was hard to breathe. But Pharaoh remained stubborn.

5. Disease on Livestock: Many of the animals in Egypt got sick and died, but still, Pharaoh would not listen.

6. Boils: Painful sores broke out on people's skin, but Pharaoh's heart remained hard.

7. Hail: Hail, mixed with fire, rained down from the sky and destroyed crops and animals.
